To Determine the Value of g by Kater's Pendulum

■ Theory:

Introduction to the Compound Pendulum:

A Kater's Pendulum is a physical (compound) pendulum designed to determine the acceleration due to gravity (g) with high precision. Unlike a simple pendulum, which assumes a point mass suspended by a massless string, a compound pendulum has its mass distributed along its length. This distribution makes it more realistic but mathematically more complex. For any rigid body oscillating freely about a horizontal axis under gravity, the time period (T) is given by:

T = 2π * √(I / (m * g * d))

where:
• I = moment of inertia about the point of suspension
• m = total mass of the pendulum
• d = distance from the point of suspension to the center of gravity (C.G.)
• g = acceleration due to gravity

The Equivalent Length Concept:

Using the Parallel Axis Theorem, the moment of inertia about any suspension point can be expressed as:
I = IG + m * d² = m * (k² + d²)

where IG = moment of inertia about the center of gravity, and k = radius of gyration. Substituting this into the time period equation:

T = 2π * √((k² + d²) / (g * d))

Comparing this with the simple pendulum formula, we can define an equivalent length of simple pendulum (Leq) as:

Leq = (k² + d²) / d = d + (k² / d)

This shows that the time period depends on both the distance to the center of gravity AND the distribution of mass.

The Theorem of Reversibility:

The fundamental principle behind Kater's Pendulum is the theorem of reversibility. Consider two points of suspension on opposite sides of the center of gravity, at distances l₁ and l₂ from the C.G. The time periods about these points are:

T₁ = 2π * √((k² + l₁²) / (g * l₁))
T₂ = 2π * √((k² + l₂²) / (g * l₂))

If these time periods are made equal (T₁ = T₂), then mathematically:
(k² + l₁²) / l₁ = (k² + l₂²) / l₂

Solving this equation leads to: k² = l₁ * l₂

Substituting this back, the equivalent length becomes:
Leq = (l₁ * l₂ + l₁²) / l₁ = l₁ + l₂

This is the key result: when the time periods about both knife-edges are equal, the distance between them (l₁ + l₂) is exactly the length of an equivalent simple pendulum. This eliminates the need to know the moment of inertia or radius of gyration.

Bessel's Formula: The Working Equation:

In practice, it is extremely difficult to make T₁ and T₂ exactly equal. Therefore, we use Bessel's formula, which gives accurate results even when the time periods are slightly different.

--- Page 3 ---
g = (8 * π²) / [ (T₁² + T₂²) / (l₁ + l₂) + (T₁² – T₂²) / (l₁ – l₂) ]

where:
• T₁ = Time period about knife-edge K₁
• T₂ = Time period about knife-edge K₂
• l₁ = Distance from C.G. to knife-edge K₁
• l₂ = Distance from C.G. to knife-edge K₂

Conditions for Accurate Measurement:

For the theoretical formula to yield accurate results, certain conditions must be maintained:

  1. Small Amplitude Oscillations: The pendulum must be displaced by less than 4° to ensure simple harmonic motion. Larger amplitudes introduce errors in the time period.
  2. Rigid Support: The knife-edges must rest on a perfectly rigid support to prevent energy loss or transfer of motion to the support.
  3. Vertical Plane Oscillation: The pendulum must oscillate in a strictly vertical plane without any conical or elliptical motion.
  4. Minimum External Disturbances: Air currents, vibrations, and other environmental factors must be minimized as they affect the time period.

1. Aim:

To determine the value of gravitational acceleration (g) using Kater's Reversible Pendulum.

2. Apparatus:

Kater's Pendulum, sharp knife-edge support, digital stopwatch, and a meter scale.

3. Working Formula:

The value of acceleration due to gravity (g) is calculated using Bessel's formula:

(8 * π²) / g = (T₁² + T₂²) / (l₁ + l₂) + (T₁² – T₂²) / (l₁ – l₂)

Where:
• T₁ and T₂ are the time periods about the knife-edges K₁ and K₂.
• l₁ and l₂ are distances of the knife-edges from the center of gravity (C.G.).

Calculations:

T₁ = 1.701 S    ⇒    T₁² = 2.8934
T₂ = 1.754 S    ⇒    T₂² = 3.0765

Calculating the first term:
(T₁² + T₂²) / (l₁ + l₂) = (2.8934 + 3.0765) / 0.993 = 5.9699 / 0.993 = 6.012 (as per notebook)

Calculating the second term:
(T₁² – T₂²) / (l₁ – l₂) = (2.8934 – 3.0765) / –0.069 = (–0.1831) / –0.069 = 2.654

Finding g:
(8 * π²) / g = 6.012 + 2.654 = 8.666
g = (8 * (3.1416)²) / 8.666 = (8 * 9.8696) / 8.666 = 78.9568 / 8.666
g ≈ 9.11 m/s²

Precautions:

  1. The Pendulum was made to oscillate in a strictly vertical plane to avoid any rotational errors.
  2. The amplitude of oscillation was kept very small (under 4°) to satisfy the condition for simple harmonic motion.
  3. Precautions were taken to ensure there were no air currents (fans were switched off) during the timing measurements.
  4. The knife-edges were sharp and properly aligned to minimize friction.
  5. Multiple readings were taken for each time period to improve accuracy.

Sources of Error:

  • Air resistance and damping effects
  • Parallax error in measuring distances l₁ and l₂
  • Human reaction time in starting/stopping the stopwatch
  • Imperfect rigidity of the support
  • Slight deviations from purely vertical oscillation

Result:

The acceleration due to gravity (g) determined using Kater's Pendulum is 9.11 m/s².

The standard value of g is 9.8 m/s². The percentage error is approximately 7.04%, which may be attributed to experimental uncertainties and environmental factors.

Conclusion:

Kater's Pendulum provides a reliable method for determining the acceleration due to gravity by utilizing the theorem of reversibility and Bessel's formula. The experiment demonstrates that even when exact equality of time periods is not achieved, accurate results can still be obtained through proper mathematical treatment. The value obtained (9.11 m/s²) is reasonably close to the standard value, confirming the validity of the method.
