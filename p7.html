Determination of Rigidity modulus of the material of a Wire by Dynamic Method

Fundamental concept of Rigidity Modulus

The rigidity modulus, denoted by eta (η), is one of the three fundamental elastic constants of a material. It quantifies the material's resistance to shear deformation. When a body is subjected to a tangential force per unit area (Shear stress), it undergoes a change in shape without change in volume. The ratio of shear stress to shear strain, within the elastic limit, is defined as the rigidity modulus. Mathematically: η = Shear stress / Shear strain

For a wire under torsion, each transverse section rotates relative to the next, producing shear strain through the material. The rigidity modulus, therefore, represents the intrinsic stiffness of the material against twisting forces.

Torsional oscillations and Restoring Couple

When a cylindrical body suspended by a wire is twisted through an angle, the wire gets deformed. Due to elasticity, internal molecular forces arise within the wire that tend to restore it to its untwisted state. These forces manifest as a restoring couple or torque.

For small twists, this restoring torque (τ) is found to be directly proportional to the angular displacement (θ). This linear relationship is the hallmark of elastic behavior within the Hooke's law regime.

τ ∝ θ    ⇒    τ = –c θ

The negative sign indicates that the torque acts opposite to the direction of angular displacement, attempting to bring the system back to equilibrium. The constant of proportionality c is termed the Torsional constant of the wire.

The Torsional Constant (c) – A Detailed Analysis

The torsional constant represents the restoring couple per unit twist of the wire. From elasticity theory, for a wire of length l, radius r, and rigidity modulus η:

c = (π * η * r⁴) / (2 * l)
  • • Material Dependence: c ∝ η – stiffer materials produce stronger restoring couple.
  • • Geometric Dependence:
    • Longer wires are easier to twist (c ∝ 1/l).
    • Extremely sensitive to radius: doubling radius increases c by 16 times (c ∝ r⁴).

Nature of Motion (SHM)

When the cylinder is twisted and released, the restoring torque τ = –cθ causes angular oscillations. From rotational dynamics:

τ = I * α = I * (d²θ/dt²)
Equating: –cθ = I * (d²θ/dt²)
Rearranging: (d²θ/dt²) + (c/I) * θ = 0

This is the differential equation for angular Simple Harmonic Motion with angular frequency: ω = √(c / I)

Derivation of Working Formula:

The time period of angular SHM is:

T = 2π / ω = 2π * √(I / c)

Squaring both sides: T² = (4π² * I) / c
Substituting c = (π * η * r⁴) / (2 * l):

η = (8π * I * l) / (r⁴ * T²)

1) Aim:

To determine the modulus of rigidity η of the material of a given wire using a torsional Pendulum (dynamic method).

2) Apparatus Required:

(i) Torsional Pendulum (Solid cylinder & experimental wire). (ii) Vernier calipers. (iii) Screw gauge. (iv) Stopwatch. (v) Meter scale. (vi) Physical balance.

3) Theory & Formula:

For a torsional Pendulum, the time period T is given by:

T = 2π √(I / c)

Squaring both sides: T² = 4π² (I / c)    ⇒    c = (4π² I) / T²

Where:
• I = Moment of inertia of the cylinder about axis = (½) M R²
• c = Torsional couple per unit twist = (π η r⁴) / (2 l)

Equating & simplifying:

η = (4π M R²) / (r⁴ * l * T²)

Where:
• M = Mass of cylinder (kg)
• R = Radius of cylinder (m)
• l = Length of wire (m)
• r = Radius of wire (m)
• T = Time Period (s)

Precautions:

For Rigidity modulus Experiment:

  1. The experimental wire must be perfectly cylindrical, free from kinks, bends, or any permanent twists that would introduce initial stresses.
  2. The amplitude of torsional oscillations should be kept small (within 5–10 degrees) to ensure validity of SHM assumptions and to remain within the elastic limit of the wire.
  3. The suspended cylinder must oscillate in a purely horizontal plane without any lateral swinging or wobbling, ensuring pure torsional oscillations.
  4. The wire should be clamped vertically and firmly at the upper end to prevent any energy dissipation through the support.
  5. Multiple readings should be taken for time period and dimensions to minimize random errors.

Sources of Error:

  • Air resistance causing damping of oscillations.
  • Imperfect clamping leading to energy loss.
  • Parallax error in reading the scales.
  • Human reaction time error in stopwatch operation.
  • Non-uniformity in wire diameter.
  • Lateral oscillations mixed with torsional oscillations.

Conclusion:

The rigidity modulus of the given wire was successfully determined using the torsional pendulum method. The obtained value, η = 7.60 × 10¹¹ N/m², is consistent with the expected range for metallic wires. The experiment demonstrates the relationship between torsional oscillations, wire dimensions, and material properties, validating the theoretical formula derived from elasticity and SHM principles.
