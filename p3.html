3. Theory

3.1 Spring Constant (k) – Static Method:
According to Hooke's Law, within elastic limit, the stress is proportional to strain. For a spring, this means the extension (Δl) produced is directly proportional to the applied force (F).
F ∝ Δl    →    F = k Δl
where:
• F = Applied force (N)
• k = spring constant (N/m)
• Δl = Extension (m)
When a mass m is suspended from the spring, the force acting is: F = mg

Therefore, mg = k Δl
k = mg / Δl

If we plot a graph between Load (mg) on Y-axis and Extension (Δl) on X-axis, we get a straight line.
The slope of this line gives the spring constant k.
Slope = F/Δl = k

3.2 Acceleration due to Gravity – Dynamic Method:
When the spring with suspended mass is slightly pulled down and released, it executes vertical oscillations.
This motion is Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM) because the restoring force is proportional to the displacement.
The time period T of oscillation is given by: T = 2π √(m/k)
where:
T = Time Period (s)
m = mass suspended (kg)

Squaring both sides:
T² = 4π² (m/k)
k = (4π² m) / T²

From the static method, we have k = mg/Δl.
Equating both expressions: mg/Δl = (4π² m)/T²
Cancelling m from both sides: g/Δl = (4π²)/T²
g = (4π² Δl) / T²

If we plot a graph between T² on Y-axis and mass (m) on X-axis, the slope will be:
Slope S = T²/m = (4π²)/k
k = (4π²)/Slope

And using the relation k = mg/Δl , we get:
g = (4π²) / (Slope × (Δl/F))
where (Δl/F) is the reciprocal of the slope from the static graph.

6. Calculations

6.1 Calculation of spring constant (k):
From Graph 1, slope = 14.0 N/m.
Since Slope = k, therefore k = 14.0 N/m

6.2 Calculation of Acceleration due to Gravity (g):
From Graph 2, slope S = 3.571 s²/kg.
Using formula g = 4π²k / S
= (4 × (3.1416)² × 14.0) / 3.571
= (4 × 9.8696 × 14) / 3.571
= (552.6976) / 3.571
= 9.86 m/s²

7. Result

The spring constant (k) of the given helical spring is 14.0 N/m.
The acceleration due to gravity (g) is calculated to be 9.86 m/s².
The standard value of g is 9.8 m/s².
The percentage error is approximately 0.61%.

8. Precautions

  • (i) Ensure the spring is not loaded beyond its elastic limit. The spring should return to its original length when the load is removed.
  • (ii) While taking scale readings, the eye should be at the same horizontal level as the pointer to avoid parallax error.
  • (iii) Always take readings for both increasing and decreasing loads and take their average. This eliminates errors due to friction or hysteresis in the spring.
  • (iv) Ensure the oscillations are purely vertical. The spring should not swing like a pendulum.
  • (v) The amplitude of oscillation should be small (within elastic limit) to maintain SHM.
  • (vi) A light pointer attached to the spring helps in accurate measurement of extension.

9. Sources of Error

  • (i) Friction at the pointer on support may affect readings.
  • (ii) The spring may have some permanent set (hysteresis).
  • (iii) Parallax error in scale reading.
  • (iv) Human reaction time error in starting/stopping the stopwatch.
  • (v) Air resistance may slightly affect the time period.
  • (vi) Error in judging exact position of pointer against scale.

10. Conclusion

The motion of a helical spring follows Hooke's Law and Simple Harmonic Motion principles.
The spring constant of the given helical spring is 14.0 N/m.
The acceleration due to gravity at the place is 9.86 m/s² which is very close to the standard value of 9.8 m/s².
The small percentage error of 0.61% indicates that the experiment was performed with good accuracy.
